History

Palawan has a rich and colorful history that spans thousands of years. It is believed that the earliest human settlers in Palawan were the Tabon Caves Man, who lived in the island some 50,000 years ago. Over the centuries, Palawan has been home to various indigenous groups such as the Tagbanua, Palawano, and Batak tribes, who have maintained their unique cultural traditions and way of life despite the influences of outside forces.

In the 16th century, the Spanish colonizers arrived in Palawan and established the town of Taytay, which became an important center of commerce and trade. The island later became a province of the Philippine Islands during the American colonial period, and played a significant role in the country's struggle for independence from colonial rule. Today, Palawan is a thriving and diverse province that continues to preserve and celebrate its rich cultural heritage and history.

Prehistoric Era

The earliest known human presence in Palawan dates back to the prehistoric era, as evidenced by the discovery of the Tabon Caves. These caves contain artifacts, human remains, and evidence of early human habitation, including rock art and tools. It is believed that the early inhabitants of Palawan were Austronesian-speaking people who migrated from the Asian mainland and other parts of the Philippines.

World War II

During World War II, Palawan was occupied by Japanese forces, who used the province as a base for their military operations in the Philippines. In 1944, American forces began an offensive to liberate Palawan, which resulted in the infamous Palawan Massacre. Over 100 American prisoners of war were burned alive by the Japanese in a tunnel in Puerto Princesa. This event, along with other atrocities committed by the Japanese in the Philippines, contributed to the Allied decision to drop atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki.
